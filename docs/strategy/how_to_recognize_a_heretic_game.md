# How to Recognize a Heretic Game

Heretic games are not defined by strange mechanics — they are defined by a hidden reversal of meaning.

The challenge is not identifying evil players — it is recognizing that **winning the game may mean the opposite of what you think**.

---

## 🧠 Core Principle

> **A Heretic game feels solvable — but the “correct” solution may lead to the wrong outcome.**

---

## 🎯 Why This Matters

If you do not recognize Heretic:

- you may solve the game perfectly and still lose
- you may execute the Demon and hand victory to evil
- you may trust a “correct” world that leads to the wrong result

Recognizing Heretic changes:

- how you evaluate endgames
- how you interpret certainty
- how you decide whether to act

---

# 🧩 Step 1 — Look for Suspiciously Clean Solutions

In normal games:

- clean solutions are good
- certainty is rewarded
- solving the game leads to victory

In Heretic games:

- the game may appear solved
- the final world may look correct
- but acting on it feels strangely risky

---

## ⚠️ Key Rule

> **If the game looks perfectly solved but still feels dangerous, question the win condition.**

---

# 🔍 Step 2 — Watch for Endgame Discomfort

Heretic reveals itself most strongly near the end.

Signs include:

- hesitation before executing the “obvious Demon”
- players questioning whether they should act
- a feeling that the final move might be wrong

---

## 🧠 What to look for

- doubt about executing the final target
- players proposing “what if we shouldn’t win like this?”
- resistance to closing the game

---

## ⚠️ Key Rule

> **Heretic creates doubt not about _who_ is evil — but about _what to do about it_.**

---

# 🔄 Step 3 — Evaluate Narrative Pressure

In Heretic games:

- players may introduce the idea that winning is unsafe
- doubt spreads through discussion, not mechanics
- the game becomes psychologically unstable

---

## 🧠 What this means

Unlike Legion:

- Heretic does not distort structure
- it distorts **decision-making**

---

## ⚠️ Key Rule

> **If the table is unsure whether executing is correct, Heretic may be in play.**

---

# 🧠 Step 4 — Compare Risk vs Reward

Ask:

- does executing clearly benefit good?
- or does it feel like it could backfire?

---

## 🧠 Example

A player is confidently identified as the Demon:

- in a normal game → execute and win
- in a Heretic game → executing may lose the game

---

## ⚠️ Key Rule

> **Heretic turns “safe” decisions into potentially losing ones.**

---

# 🔄 Step 5 — Consider Timing

Heretic is strongest when:

- the game is close to resolution
- one team feels confident
- the final decision is imminent

---

## ⚠️ Key Rule

> **If doubt appears at the exact moment the game should end, it may not be accidental.**

---

# ⚠️ Common Mistakes

### ❌ Ignoring the possibility entirely

Heretic only matters if you consider it.

---

### ❌ Overcorrecting too early

Do not assume Heretic just because the game is messy.

---

### ❌ Trusting certainty blindly

Certainty is exactly what Heretic punishes.

---

### ❌ Freezing completely

Indecision can lose the game just as easily as the wrong decision.

---

# 🎯 Practical Heuristic

When you suspect Heretic, ask:

1. Does the game appear solved too cleanly?
2. Does executing the “correct” target feel risky?
3. Is there hesitation at the point where the game should end?
4. Would reversing the win condition explain the tension?
5. Are we about to make a move that could backfire?

---

# 🔄 What To Do If It Is Heretic

If Heretic is likely:

- slow down before final executions
- question whether “winning” is actually correct
- consider alternative outcomes
- avoid blindly following certainty

---

## ⚠️ Key Rule

> **In a Heretic game, the most confident move may be the most dangerous one.**

---

# 🧠 Final Insight

> **Heretic is not revealed through broken logic — it is revealed when perfect logic leads to doubt.**
