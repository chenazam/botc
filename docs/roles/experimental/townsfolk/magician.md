# Magician (Experimental)

**Role Type:** Townsfolk  
**Ability:** The Demon thinks you are a Minion. Minions think you are a Demon.

---

## 🧠 Overview

Magician is a role that **distorts evil’s information**, not good’s.

You provide:

- misinformation to the evil team
- disruption of coordination
- hidden protection through confusion

Your power lies in:

- making evil misidentify each other
- breaking reliable communication within the evil team
- creating mistakes that look like normal play

---

# 🟢 Playing Magician (Good)

## 🎯 Your Role

You are not just a Townsfolk — you are:

> **A hidden disruptor of evil coordination**

---

## 🟢 Day 1 Priorities

### 1. Play like a normal good role

Default strong play:

> “I’ll just play my role normally.”

Why:

- your ability works passively
- drawing attention reduces your impact
- evil confusion is strongest when unnoticed

---

### 2. Stay unpredictable but consistent

You should:

- avoid being too easy to categorize
- maintain believable behavior
- avoid extreme or risky claims

---

### 3. Observe interactions

You should watch for:

- strange coordination between players
- odd support patterns
- players misreading each other

---

## 🧠 Mid-game Strategy

### Let confusion develop

Move from:

> “I need to create impact.”

to:

> **“My impact is already happening.”**

---

### Use subtle influence

You can:

- encourage misreads
- support conflicting narratives
- avoid clarifying too cleanly

---

### Stay alive if possible

Your ability:

- continues to affect evil as long as you are alive
- creates more value over time

---

## ⚠️ Common Mistakes (Good)

### ❌ Overplaying the role

Magician is strongest when invisible.

---

### ❌ Claiming Magician early

This removes all confusion for evil.

---

### ❌ Trying to force chaos

The confusion happens naturally.

---

# 😈 Bluffing Magician (Evil)

## 🎯 Your Role

As evil, your goal is to:

> **Use the Magician effect to explain miscoordination**

---

## 🟢 Why Magician is a strong bluff

- explains inconsistent evil behavior
- justifies lack of coordination
- creates doubt about team alignment
- fits naturally into many worlds

---

## 🧩 Core Bluff

> “Magician would explain why this feels off.”

---

## 🔑 Step 1: Introduce Magician as a possibility

### 🟢 Good approach

- connect it to observed misplays
- use it to explain confusion
- let others reach the conclusion

---

### 🚫 Avoid

- claiming Magician too early
- using it without supporting evidence

---

## 🔑 Step 2: Use it to destabilize reads

### 🟢 Safe choices

- question player connections
- challenge assumptions about evil coordination

---

### 🔴 Risky choices

- overusing Magician as a catch-all explanation
- contradicting established worlds too strongly

---

## 🔑 Step 3: Control interpretation

You should guide the table toward:

- uncertainty about who knows what
- doubt about alignment signals
- mistrust of coordination patterns

---

### Example plays

- “Magician could explain why these players don’t line up.”
- “If evil is confused, that changes how we read this.”

---

## 🔄 Mid-game as evil

### Stay flexible

If the bluff weakens:

- reduce emphasis
- pivot to normal explanations
- keep only residual doubt

---

### Use your claim strategically

- disrupt trust between players
- weaken social reads
- blur alignment clarity

---

## ⚠️ Common Mistakes (Evil)

### ❌ Overcommitting

Magician is best as a supporting explanation.

---

### ❌ Using it too early

It works best after confusion appears.

---

### ❌ Making it the only explanation

It should support, not replace, other worlds.

---

# 🧠 Advanced Notes

## 🔀 Information asymmetry

Magician affects:

- what the Demon thinks
- what Minions think

but not:

- good players directly

---

## 🧩 Social effect

Magician creates:

- misaligned evil coordination
- subtle inconsistencies
- believable confusion

---

## 🧠 Strategic impact

Magician shifts the game from:

- **reading evil coordination**

to:

- **questioning whether coordination is reliable**

---

## 🧠 Key Insight

> **Magician does not create chaos — it makes coordination unreliable.**
