# Fortune Teller (Trouble Brewing)

**Role Type:** Townsfolk  
**Ability:** Each night, choose 2 players: you learn if either is a Demon. There is a good player that registers as a Demon to you.

---

## 🧠 Overview

The Fortune Teller is a **recurring information role** that detects the **presence of the Demon within chosen pairs**, but with built-in uncertainty via a **Red Herring**.

You provide:

- a yes/no result indicating whether at least one of two chosen players registers as the Demon

Your power lies in:

- narrowing down Demon candidates over time
- triangulating information across multiple checks
- forcing the Demon to play around your selections

---

## 🗂️ Tags

- recurring-info

---

# 🟢 Playing Fortune Teller (Good)

## 🎯 Your Role

You are not just detecting the Demon — you are:

> **Building overlapping checks to isolate the true Demon despite misinformation**

---

## 🟢 Day 1 Priorities

### 1. Share your information (often early, but optional)

Default strong play:

> “I’m Fortune Teller, I checked A and B and got a yes/no.”

Why:

- your info benefits from collaboration
- others can help interpret your results
- early sharing builds trust and coordination

---

### 2. Plan your checks carefully

You should:

- include at least one consistent reference player
- rotate the second target
- build overlapping data points

---

### 3. Track your results clearly

You must remember:

- which pairs you checked
- what result you got
- how those results interact

---

## 🧠 Mid-game Strategy

### Use triangulation

You are trying to:

> **narrow down which player causes repeated “yes” results**

Example:

- A+B → yes
- A+C → yes
- B+C → no

→ likely A is the Demon (or Red Herring)

---

### Account for the Red Herring

One good player will always:

- register as Demon to you

This means:

- a consistent “yes” target might be false
- you must compare across multiple players

---

### Combine with other information

Fortune Teller works best with:

- Undertaker confirmations
- Empath readings
- execution results
- claim consistency

---

## ⚠️ Common Mistakes (Good)

### ❌ Assuming “yes” means Demon

- it may be the Red Herring

---

### ❌ Not using overlapping checks

- isolated checks are weak
- patterns are essential

---

### ❌ Tunneling one player too early

- you must test alternatives

---

# 😈 Bluffing Fortune Teller (Evil)

## 🎯 Your Role

As evil, your goal is to:

> **Create believable yes/no patterns that support a false Demon world**

---

## 🟢 Why Fortune Teller is a strong bluff

- ambiguous results
- Red Herring explains inconsistencies
- flexible and evolving
- difficult to disprove immediately

---

## 🧩 Core Bluff

> “I’m Fortune Teller, I checked A and B and got a yes/no.”

---

## 🔑 Step 1: Choose your targets carefully

### 🟢 Include a consistent reference player

- makes your checks look structured
- helps build believable patterns

---

### 🟢 Choose targets that support your narrative

- include suspicious players
- avoid contradicting strong info

---

### 🚫 Avoid

- impossible combinations
- obvious contradictions with known info

---

## 🔑 Step 2: Control your results

### 🟢 Use “yes” strategically

- to frame good players
- to reinforce suspicion

---

### 🟢 Use “no” strategically

- to clear allies
- to appear helpful

---

## 🔑 Step 3: Leverage the Red Herring

If contradictions appear:

> “There must be a Red Herring involved.”

---

### Example plays

- “I keep getting yes on A, but that could be the Red Herring.”
- “This pattern suggests one of these two is the Demon.”

---

## 🔄 Mid-game as evil

### Stay flexible

If your results don’t fit:

- adjust your pattern
- introduce Red Herring explanations
- avoid rigid conclusions

---

### Shape the Demon narrative

You can:

- protect your Demon
- frame good players
- guide executions

---

## ⚠️ Common Mistakes (Evil)

### ❌ Inconsistent check patterns

- random checks look suspicious

---

### ❌ Ignoring your own logic

- your results must form a believable pattern

---

### ❌ Overcommitting to one suspect

- leave room for Red Herring explanations

---

# 🧠 Advanced Notes

## 🔀 Red Herring mechanics

One good player will always:

- register as Demon to the Fortune Teller

This creates:

- persistent false positives
- overlapping misleading patterns

---

## 🧩 Check structure

Strong Fortune Teller play often uses:

- a fixed reference player
- rotating second targets

---

## 🧠 Social impact

Fortune Teller often:

- drives Demon suspicion
- creates strong but uncertain worlds
- forces players to respond to structured accusations

---

## 🧠 Key Insight

> **Fortune Teller is about patterns, not individual results — and one of those patterns is always false.**
