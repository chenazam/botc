# Sailor (Bad Moon Rising)

**Role Type:** Townsfolk  
**Ability:** Each night, choose a player: either you or they are drunk until dusk. You can’t die.

---

## 🧠 Overview

The Sailor is a **protection role with built-in uncertainty**, capable of preventing death while simultaneously introducing **drunkenness into the game**.

You provide:

- strong survivability
- targeted protection
- controlled (but uncertain) disruption

Your power lies in:

- denying Demon kills
- influencing night outcomes
- forcing the game to account for hidden drunkenness

---

# 🟢 Playing Sailor (Good)

## 🎯 Your Role

You are not just protecting players — you are:

> **Controlling survival while introducing uncertainty into the night**

---

## 🟢 Day 1 Priorities

### 1. Decide your claim strategy

Default strong play:

> “I’m a role that can influence survival at night — I may or may not confirm later.”

Why:

- full early claims can make you a target
- your ability is powerful but not cleanly confirmable
- ambiguity is part of your strength

---

### 2. Choose early targets carefully

You should:

- consider protecting strong information roles
- think about who the Demon is likely to target
- balance protection vs. risk of drunkenness

---

### 3. Accept uncertainty immediately

You must assume:

- either you or your target is drunk
- you may not be protecting who you think
- your own reasoning may be compromised

---

## 🧠 Mid-game Strategy

### Work with uncertain outcomes

Move from:

> “I protected them, so they should be alive.”

to:

> **“If they lived, what are all the possible explanations?”**

---

### Use survival patterns

Repeated nights can reveal:

- whether protection is likely working
- whether drunkenness is affecting outcomes
- whether the Demon is avoiding your targets

---

### Coordinate carefully

You can:

- align with other protective or information roles
- test hypotheses across multiple nights

But:

- over-coordination can amplify incorrect assumptions

---

## ⚠️ Common Mistakes (Good)

### ❌ Assuming your protection worked

You may be the one who is drunk — your ability may not function as expected.

---

### ❌ Treating survival as confirmation

A player surviving does not prove they are good or protected.

---

### ❌ Ignoring your own drunkenness

Your decisions and conclusions may be based on incorrect assumptions.

---

# 😈 Bluffing Sailor (Evil)

## 🎯 Your Role

As evil, your goal is to:

> **Explain unusual survival patterns while introducing controlled uncertainty**

---

## 🟢 Why Sailor is a strong bluff

- explains why players survive unexpectedly
- introduces built-in uncertainty (“maybe I was drunk”)
- fits naturally into chaotic BMR night patterns
- is difficult to fully verify

---

## 🧩 Core Bluff

> “I’m Sailor — I picked X last night. Either I was drunk or they were.”

---

## 🔑 Step 1: Choose believable targets

### 🟢 Good targets

- players who survived unexpectedly
- players who are plausible Demon targets
- players already central to the game

---

### ⚠️ Risky targets

- players whose survival is already explained
- players who contradict your narrative

---

### 🚫 Avoid

- claims that conflict with clear death patterns
- over-explaining outcomes too precisely

---

## 🔑 Step 2: Use ambiguity correctly

### 🟢 Good approach

- lean into uncertainty
- allow multiple explanations
- avoid committing to exact outcomes

---

### 🔴 Risky approach

- claiming too much certainty about who was drunk
- trying to “prove” your protection worked

---

## 🔑 Step 3: Shape night interpretation

You should guide the table toward:

- accepting unclear survival outcomes
- questioning whether protection or drunkenness occurred
- avoiding clean conclusions

---

### Example plays

- “If they lived, maybe I hit them — or maybe I was drunk.”
- “That night doesn’t have a clean explanation.”

---

## 🔄 Mid-game as evil

### Stay flexible

If your bluff weakens:

- shift from “I protected them” to “this night was messy”
- lean into general uncertainty
- avoid being tied to one explanation

---

### Use your claim strategically

- explain unexpected survival
- protect a teammate indirectly
- create doubt in otherwise clean worlds

---

## ⚠️ Common Mistakes (Evil)

### ❌ Overcommitting to outcomes

Sailor is strong because it’s ambiguous — don’t remove that.

---

### ❌ Ignoring night consistency

Your claims must still broadly fit the game’s death patterns.

---

### ❌ Forcing explanations

Let the uncertainty exist — forcing clarity weakens the bluff.

---

# 🧠 Advanced Notes

## 🔀 Drunkenness interaction

Each night:

- either you or your target becomes drunk

This means:

- your ability may fail
- your reasoning may be flawed
- outcomes are inherently uncertain

---

## 🧩 Survival vs. function

Even if drunk:

- you may still survive
- but your protection may not apply correctly

This creates layered ambiguity in night outcomes.

---

## 🧠 Sailor in BMR specifically

In a script already defined by:

- unusual deaths
- protection
- volatile nights

The Sailor amplifies:

- interpretive difficulty
- uncertainty around survival
- complexity of night analysis

---

## 🧠 Key Insight

> **Sailor is not about guaranteeing survival — it is about making survival impossible to interpret cleanly.**
