# Building and Testing Worlds (Trouble Brewing)

Trouble Brewing is a script built around **clean information and logical deduction**.

To play it well, you must move beyond individual claims and instead:

> **Build complete worlds — and test which one survives.**

---

## 🧠 Core Principle

> **Do not ask “who is lying?” — ask “which full world still works?”**

---

## 🎯 Why This Matters

In Trouble Brewing:

- most information is reliable
- inconsistencies usually mean something is wrong
- the game is solvable through logic

But only if you:

- consider the _entire game state_
- not just isolated pieces of information

---

# 🧩 Step 1 — Build a World

A “world” is a complete explanation of the game:

- who is good
- who is evil
- which roles are in play
- how all information fits together

---

## 🧠 Example

You might build:

- Empath is true → neighbors are evil
- Washerwoman confirms Librarian
- Librarian saw Drunk

👉 That creates a _coherent world_

---

## ⚠️ Key Rule

> **A world must explain ALL information — not just some of it.**

---

# 🔍 Step 2 — Test for Contradictions

Once you have a world:

> **Try to break it.**

Ask:

- does every piece of information still make sense?
- are role interactions consistent?
- does outsider count match setup logic?

---

## 🧩 Common breaking points

- Empath conflicts with Investigator
- Fortune Teller conflicts with confirmed roles
- outsider count does not match Baron

---

## ⚠️ Key Rule

> **If one piece of information breaks the world, the world is wrong.**

---

# 🔄 Step 3 — Maintain Multiple Worlds

Do not commit too early.

Instead:

- track 2–3 possible worlds
- compare them over time
- eliminate them as contradictions appear

---

## 🧠 Good practice

- World A → Poisoner scenario
- World B → Baron scenario
- World C → Spy misinformation

---

## ⚠️ Key Rule

> **Trouble Brewing rewards patience — not early certainty.**

---

# ⚠️ Common Mistakes

### ❌ Tunnel vision

Locking into one world too early.

---

### ❌ Ignoring contradictions

Explaining away inconsistencies instead of rejecting the world.

---

### ❌ Overvaluing single roles

No single role should define the entire game.

---

# 🎯 Practical Heuristic

When evaluating a world:

1. Does it explain all information?
2. Does it match outsider count?
3. Does it require fewer assumptions than alternatives?
4. Does it stay consistent over time?

---

# 🧠 Final Insight

> **In Trouble Brewing, the correct world is the one that breaks last — not the one that looks best first.**
